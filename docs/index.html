StackBot: Diffusion Pipeline for Stacking Blocks

Mya Schroder*, Xinning He*
University of Wisconsin Madison
*Indicates Equal Contribution

Abstract

We present a teleoperation-based data collection and imitation learning pipeline for the Tesollo DG-3F three-finger gripper mounted on a Franka Panda robotic arm. Using this pipeline, we collect a dataset of block-stacking demonstrations in which the robot must grasp a block, lift it, and precisely place it atop a second block. We then train a Diffusion Policy model on the collected demonstrations. Our experiments show that the learned policy successfully generalizes block-stacking behavior from a modest number of human demonstrations, validating both the expressiveness of Diffusion Policy for dexterous manipulation and the practicality of our teleoperation pipeline for real-robot data collection with multi-fingered end effectors.

Introduction

Diffusion policies have recently shown strong performance in visuomotor control, particularly in dexterous manipulation tasks. These methods enable robots to learn complex, multimodal behaviors directly from data, without requiring explicit modeling of system dynamics. A common approach to collecting such data is through human teleoperation, which provides an intuitive and scalable way to generate demonstrations for robot learning. By allowing operators to directly control the robot, teleoperation captures rich task-relevant behaviors that can be used to train policies. Our research questions are presented below:

RQ1. How can we adapt a diffusion policy architecture to support the control of a bimanual robot system with 3-finger dexterous robot grippers?

RQ2. How can we design a teleoperation system to collect high-quality dexterous demonstration data?

Diffusion Policy Pipeline

Our Diffusion Policy pipeline includes three stages. First, wrist and finger movements are tracked and retargeted into robot end-effector pose and gripper joint positions. Second, these retargeted commands are used to teleoperate the robot in simulation and block-stacking demonstrations. Third, the collected data is used to train a low-dimension diffusion policy.

Stage 1: Finger & Wrist Retargeting

To track the wrist and finger positions, we used the MediaPipe Hand Tracking and Dex Retargeting from the AnyTeleop Project [1]. We used a RGB-depth camera (Realsense d435) to localize the wrist relative to the camera frame using the depth data.The wrist pose is linearly scaled and mapped to the robot end-effector pose so that the robot can reach the entire workspace. We also applied a One Euro Filter [3], a low-pass filter that reduces jitter while minimizing lag, to smooth the wrist-tracking data.

To retarget the finger tracking into gripper joint positions, we used the Dex Retargeting optimizer. Since the Tesollo grippers have a different morphology than the human hand, we had to make two changes to the URDF to get the Dex Retargeting optimizer to work. (1) We had to to add a virtual wrist link that was closer to the margin-bottom "palm" of the gripper. (2) We had to add virtual finger tip links with adjusted orientation. The optimizer used these wrist and finger links, along with a 1.8 scaling factor parameter to perform retargeting. These changes were discovered through a tedious trial and error process.

Stage 2: Teleoperation & Data Collection

We used the end-effector pose and gripper joint positions to teleoperate the robot in Isaacsim in realtime. We found that placing the camera on the ground with the lense facing upwards tracked the hand the best. The hand tracking model was sensitive to lighting and struggled when the hand was closed due to shadows. To counteract this, we found the best lighting conditions occurred during a cloudy day with the overhead lights off and a phone flashlight facing up towards the hand.

We found teleoperation very challenging due to a lack of depth perception in simulation and a lack of tactile/force feedback. To address these challenges, we experimented with the following three teleoperation aids:
(1) Visual Guidance: We added a virtual green arrow facing down from the robot gripper to help with operator alignment to compensate for the depth-perception challenges. We also experimented with the lighting to adjust shadows. We found stronger shadows did not improve depth perception enough and instead obscured the workspace. Removing shadows was the most helpful for teleoperation.
(2) Adaptive Motion Scaling: To speed up teleoperation while allowing fine-grained movements close to the blocks, we experimented with 1:1 movement scaling for motions high above the table and reduced scaling for precision near the table. In practice, the feature introduced additional tuning complexity without enough improvement in usability to justify it, so we removed it.
(3) Stability Aids: To help with block alignment, we experimented with stability aids to lock the horizontal movement when the gripper was above the block. However, this resulted in jerky "locking" actions and would have required an optimization-based blending approach to be smooth. We ended up removing this feature because the virtual green arrow proved adequate for aiding in block alignment.

After recording the demonstrations, we removed any trials in which we failed to stack the block within approximately one minute. However, we retained demonstrations that included recovery behavior, as long as the block was successfully stacked within the allotted time.

Stage 3: Diffusion Policy Training

We adapted the Diffusion Policy [2] architecture from its original MuJoCo-based implementation to the Isaac Sim simulation platform. To accommodate the dexterous gripper, we expanded the action space from the baseline to 18 dimensions, adding 12 degrees of freedom to capture the full articulation of the hand's fingers. The observation space consisted of 19-dimensional low-level proprioceptive states, providing the policy with compact but informative sensory input. We retained the CNN-based U-Net denoising backbone from the original architecture, which iteratively refines noisy action sequences into coherent motor commands through the diffusion process.

Experiments

We ran two different experiments. The first one was for initial testing of the model and the second one was a more difficult task for testing the model robustness. In the first experiment, the task was stacking a small blue block on top of a larger red one. Here the blocks were spawned in the same place every time. We were able to achieve success with 60 demonstrations (trained for 1100 epochs with a training loss of 0.002).

In the second experiment, the task was to stack a uniform blue block onto the same-sized red block. The block positions were randomly initialized on the workspace table (~40x40cm). However, with 103 demonstrations (trained for 1250 epochs with a 0.0002 loss), the model was still not able to stack the block successfully. We infer that this is because the large workspace caused the model to operate outside its training distribution. Because of this, we opted to constrain the workspace to ~10x10cm and achieved success after 66 demonstrations (trained for 1400 epochs with a training loss of 0.001). We then combined both the large and constrained workspace datasets for a total of 169 demonstrations (trained for 900 epochs with a 0.002 loss). We used this checkpoint for our final evaluation below. We found that training further than a 0.001 loss caused overfitting which led to failure during evaluation. Our final training checkpoints are available here.

For our final evaluation in the second experiment, we ran 30 rollouts with inference at 10 HZ and used a 12-step action chunking. We measured success as being able to stack the blue block on the red block without knocking it off within 1-minute. The diffusion model achieved a 63% success rate. Note, that while the success rate appears fairly low, it is better than our own teleoperation success rate of 53%. This task is challenging because the blue block must be aligned on the red block to avoid falling off. It must also be released carefully without being knocked over, since the block width is similar to that of the gripper.

Conclusion

While our current implementation demonstrates promising results, several limitations point to clear directions for future work.

  • Observations: The policy presently relies on low-dimensional proprioceptive observations, which limits its applicability to real-world scenarios where object states cannot be directly measured; transitioning to image-based observations would enable more generalizable and deployment-ready perception.
  • Deployment: The system has only been validated in simulation, and bridging the sim-to-real gap to deploy on a physical robot remains a critical next step.
  • Hardware: The current single-arm setup constrains the complexity of manipulation tasks that can be performed, and extending the system to a bimanual coordination framework would substantially broaden its capabilities.
  • Task: The task scope is presently limited to relatively simple block stacking; scaling to a more diverse repertoire of dexterous manipulation tasks — such as tool use, or container opening — will be essential for demonstrating the full potential of the approach.
